Kloudy

User Experience / User Information Design<

Many people who try to quit smoking transfer from traditional cigarettes to E-cigarettes, vapes or other equivalent products. These new “tools” claim to contain less nicotine and be less harmful to the body. However, without accountability, smokers can be consuming the same, if not more, amount of nicotine. The Kloudy Vape and app act as accountability tools for users, as they provide users their daily nicotine consumption, the frequency of vaping sessions, and the times when they smoke. With all the collected information from the Kloudy vape, the app creates progress reports, hence providing users an effective and measurable way of quitting their habit.

The Problem

Smokers trying to quit their habit through vaping, cannot accurately measure their progress.

Research

Quantitative Research

According to the Canadian Community Health Survey (Statistics Canada, 2018), it is estimated that there are over 5 million current smokers living in Canada as of 2017. From these 5 million smokers, 68.0% of them wanted to quit (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention).


Qualitative Research

My group and I conducted over twenty interviews, asking what users would like to see in our vape and application. Our interviewees were University of Toronto students, that all had over 1+ years of actively vaping. Each interviewee varied in their reason for vaping, some were trying to quit while others vaped as a lifestyle activity.

Core Findings

Below are direct quotes from our user interviews:

“Although I can see the nicotine percentage in a single juul pod, I want to see my overall nicotine consumption in an accurate manner.”


“The ability to see the  progress of my immediate and long-term goals, would motivate me to quit smoking altogether. Small goals such as decreasing my vaping sessions to twenty a day, would motivate me to complete my long term goal of only consuming 10 mg of nicotine a month.”


“The Kloudy application should have a socialization feature, users would be able to interact with other like-minded individuals looking to quit smoking. A successful community will result in accountability partners and add gamification aspect to their journey.”


“The application should be easy to use, incorporating aesthetically pleasing graphics and designs that will elevate users’ experience.”

Breaking Down the Process

The next step was to put the knowledge from research in motion to define the app's functions, user flows and design the prototype.

Vape Functions

User Testing

University of Toronto students volunteered to test our prototypes.

Usability tests took place in CC3160 in the Communication, Culture & Technology building At the University of Toronto Mississauga.


Lo-fidelity, mid-fidelity, and high-fidelity prototypes of our “Kloudy” vape and application were administered to participants for the study. We used true experiments including keystroke recording and thinking out loud protocols. Using these experiments we were able to collect detailed results. Keystroke recording showed how users navigate through our app and the thinking aloud protocols allowed us to see and hear the associated behaviours/opinions of our product.


Furthermore, in more detailed prototypes we gave participants different scenarios. Each scenario had a list of tasks such as pairing your Kloudy vape, navigating to the “user tracking” subsection of the application, and editing “long-term goals”. The observations from each scenario, acted as guidance for our innovations.

First Prototype

User Testing Takeaways

  • Placement of Power Button

    • Users suggested to place the power button at the top of the vape. Its placement on the side of the vape, resulted in an inconvenient grip for users


  • Bluetooth Indicator

    • The LCD screen of the vape did not have a Bluetooth icon. Users wanted a lit up Bluetooth icon on the screen, once they were successfully paired to the app.


  • "Sessions" Jargon

    • Many users were confused about the "Sessions" jargon. We had to explain to our users, that a session was a "drag" of 1 to 3 seconds from the vape.

Settings

In our first prototype, we did not have a Settings page. Users wanted the ability to basic functions such as editing their profile, turning on/off sounds and notifications. We added this page in our next prototype.

Current Usage

Our first "Current Usage" page did not meet all the requirements of most users. They wanted to the targeted # of puffs to see if they were on track. The graphic visualization helped see this progress but not accurately. Moreover, users wanted the ability to view and edit goals from the "Current Usage" page. They found the process redundant to navigate back to the account page to edit this.

Second Prototype

User Testing Takeaways

  • Nicotine Intensity

    • Users found the 3 level were too simple to represent the nicotine intensity. A 5 level bar, showing the actual percentage of the nicotine intensity would have been a better reprsentation.


  • Battery percentage

    • Users wanted the percentage of the battery life, rather than just the battery icon.

Redundant Home Button

In our second prototype a major change was the addition of the "back" button on all our pages. In the previous prototype, we only had the "Kloudy" home button for users to visit other pages. 85% of users mentioned that without the "back" button, they found navigating through our app too linear.

Goals Page

Our goals page added the feature of scanning various vape juices. Users reacted positively to this change, as they found manually inputting their juice name/code too time consuming.

Community Page

The majority of our users gave our "Community" page high praise. However, they wanted more functionalities added including the ability to like and comment on their friend'statuses.

Conclusion

Final Takeaways

The Kloudy team sought to create a product that fit the needs of users looking to cut their nicotine consumption using non-cigarette alternatives. By identifying the demands of the product target market as well as conducting a slew usability evaluations, the team was able to consistently re-iterate and implement more effective design functions, ultimately leading to the release of the final Kloudy Vape and Accountability application.


Prioritizing certain iterations over others was a long process of trial and error, documenting which changes positively benefitted the users. This four month long project, resulted in users being 33% more efficient with the given tasks and 82% less mistakes when navigating (i.e. editing their long-term goals) and an increase in satisfaction with our overall design.
