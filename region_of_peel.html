Region of Peel

How developing an accessible site made me a stronger designer.

TLDR: As a Front-end Developer intern, I coded www.peelregion.ca to be more accessible, organized, and easy to use. No citizen should have to struggle to find their garbage collection schedule or where the nearest vaccination clinic is.


The lessons learned from this summer internship shaped both my career path and values as a designer.

Takeaways from my dev internship that shaped my values as a designer.

Designing for accessibility isn‚Äôt for a niche market.

In 2017, one in five (22%) of the Canadian population aged 15 years and over had one or more disabilities.


That's a lot of people. Keeping that in mind, I never want accessibility to be an after-thought. Your design should be easily digestible for people with physical, visual and/or auditory impairments.

Be wary of trends.

Colour contrast is important. I made sure all text was easily readable on the Region of Peel website.


The first time I saw a Dribble shot using Neumorphism, I was in awe and loved it. However, this practice is taking the user completely out of the equation. A user with colour blindness would not be able to differentiate buttons, backgrounds, and important info due to the low colour contrast.


No matter how cool and modern a trend is, don't forget the basic design principles.

Be kind to your developers.

Translating designs into html, css, java, etc. is hard. Tropical gradients, complex animations, and crazy drop shadows are all awesome and can help with the UI.


However, at the end of the day a developer will have to code your design. Love your devs.

What I want to reiterate.

Empathy is key.

We need more designers to be advocates for accessibility. We aren't desiging for just one uniform user but for people with unique needs and abilities.
