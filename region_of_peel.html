Region of Peel

UX / UI

Overview

Prioritizing the needs of all 1.4 million citizens

My proudest accomplishment as Web Designer for the Region of Peel, was being involved in the massive design overhaul of www.peelregion.ca.

Despite being one of Canada's largest municipalities with over 1.4 million citizens from Mississauga, Brampton and Caledon, the previous website was disorganized, had no consistent branding, and most importantly was inaccessible by people with disabilities.

The Problem

The Region of Peel website was inaccessible, disorganized, and did not comply with AODA standards (Accessibility to Ontarions with Disabilities Act).

Our Users

Designing for a diverse set of users with different needs

In 2017, one in five (22%) of the Canadian population aged 15 years and over had one or more disabilities.

Therefore, the Region of Peel site had to be accessible by individuals who had auditory, motor, visual, speech, and/or cognitive disabilities. Citizens must be able to navigate through our site using screen readers, braille keyboards, speech input, screen magnifiers, and hands-free mouse tracking.

Previous Interface

Accessibility issues with the previous interface

1. Weak Colour Contrast

Strong colour contrast is crucial for readability. Without WCAG approved contrast, people with colour blindness or impaired vision cannot view information properly. In the example shown above, citizens with colour blindness would not be able to read the Public Health phone number. The light green background does not provide enough contrast for the white text.

2. Missing Alt Text for Images

Visually impaired citizens use screen readers to hear the information on a webpage. Therefore, it is important for all images to have a useful paired alt tag. The alt tag should describe the image and its context. However, if the image is strictly for decorative purposes, the alt tag is best left empty. Without an alt tag, the screen reader will read out the useless file name of the image to the user.

3. Numerous Navigation Menus

On this page alone, there are three navigation menus. This can present the problem of information overload. Each menu has numerous pages and an overwhelming number of links. It is physically and mentally taxing for visually impaired citizens using their keyboard (instead of a mouse) to go through all the options to find their desired page.

Being wary of the number and size of your navigation menus will result in a more pleasant experience for users.

4. Confusing Information Architecture

In the example shown above, the content is disorganized. The titles, subtitles, and body text are not clear. To help citizens see the hierarchy of a page, it is vital to follow the H1, H2, H3, p, etc. protocol to organize information.

Also, the images in the body text switch side to side. For easy readability, content should always go left-to-right and up-down.

5. Unresponsive

This webpage is not responsive. Webpages and information must be able to respond to different screen sizes. Citizens viewing this webpage on their phone will have difficulty finding information, as the site is only optimized for desktops.

Self Reflection

What I Contributed

As a Junior Web designer I translated the content from the old to new website. As I completed this task I learned about key iterations for shorter user flows and ultimately a more accessible web interface. These design iterations were communicated to a digital team including my supervisor, content strategists and back-end developers.

Some specific iterations included changing the hierarchy of information (i.e.removing repetitive navigation bars and nested information), removing inaccessible buttons such as “click here” and adding descriptive tags to all images for blind citizens.
