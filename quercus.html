Quercus

User Experience / User Interface Design

Quercus is the official Learning Management System for University of Toronto students. My group and I wanted to redesign this system to improve its Calendar and Inbox functionalities. The Calendar was not being utilized by students and the Inbox was met with friction and confusion.

The Problem

The calendar and inbox functionalities of the Quercus system, are not optimized for student use.

Research

Preliminary Observations

To gather the initial thoughts of Quercus from its users, I conducted over ten interviews with current UofT students, instructors, and administrators.

Sample Questions Included:

“What platform do you use to contact your professors and peers?“


“What functionalities of the Quercus Calendar do you use?”


“What are your general thoughts of the Quercus Inbox and Calendar?”

Core Findings About the Current Inbox

Below are direct quotes from our preliminary interviews

“The Inbox feature on Quercus is quite confusing. I have always used UTORmail to contact my professors and peers. Now that I have an additional method of communication, I don’t know which platform to use. I wish UTORmail was directly integrated into Quercus.”


“I don’t use the Inbox feature at all. I find it useless, as most of my professors want me to contact them through UTORmail, using OutLook.”


“Some of my professors use the Quercus Inbox and some use the UTORmail inbox. It frustrates me to constantly check both platforms.”

Core Findings About the Current Calendar

Below are direct quotes from our preliminary interviews

“The Calendar function has so much potential to be a useful feature. However, the Calendar is not up-to-date. Most of my assignment deadlines are not posted in the Calendar, hence why I manually input them in my personal agenda.”


“I cannot rely on the Calendar to check my project deadlines. Deadlines are subject to change in each course and they are not updated to the Calendar. Currently, I am using my Calendar on my MacBook to input these dates.”


“If the Quercus Calendar had an option to sync my Google Calendar, I would definitely use it more.”

Pilot Testing

Preliminary User Testing

Although we had the initial thoughts of the Quercus system, I wanted to gain a deeper understanding of how they interacted with its functionalities.

We gave each participant two tasks using Quercus. The tasks were used to observe the user flows of participants, before our redesign. Our ultimate goal was to redesign Quercus to make these user flows and interactions more efficient.


Task 1

Participants were instructed to email their professor a question about their course material. Over 80% of the participants followed the user flow below. The majority of participants were completely unaware of the Quercus Inbox.


Task 2

Participants were instructed to input an assignment deadline into the Quercus Calendar. Over 70% of participants followed the userflow below. The other 30% of participants directly inputted the deadline into the Quercus Calendar. However, these participants still complained they would not use the Calendar as it was never up-to-date. They would much rather prefer using Google Calendar or ICal.

What the Students Wanted

I summarized the most significant functionalities of the Quercus System.

Quercus Inbox Functions

  • Communicate to professors, classmates, and peers
  • UTORmail integration
  • Automated contact information

Quercus Calendar Functions

  • Input various different tasks
  • Up-to-date assignment/project deadlines
  • Google calendar and ICal sync option

User Testing

University of Toronto students tested our prototypes.

Now that we had redesigned Quercus, I was curious about their thoughts and how they can be translated into improvements. To gather their thoughts, we instructed students to complete tasks such as inputting an assignment deadline into the Quercus Calendar.

Tools such as keystroke recording and thinking aloud protocols showed us how students navigated through our low to high-fidelity prototypes and hear their associated behaviours/opinions of the redesigns.

First Prototype

User Testing Takeaways

Quercus Inbox

UTORmail Integration

In our first prototype, we integrated UTORmail on Outlook into Quercus. Users found this iteration, a significant helpful change. Users no longer had to check two platforms to communicate with their professors. This integration allowed for participants to shorten their previous user flow.

Quercus Calendar

Google & ICal Sync Option

In the redesigned Quercus calendar, participants had the option to sync their external calendars to the system. 60% of participants use an external calendar, therefore they found this option useful. By syncing all their calendars, students could see all their personal and academic tasks in one place.

Second Prototype

User Testing Takeaways

Quercus Inbox

Email Shortcut

The letter icon in the bottom right corner was a shortcut to create an email. It was stationary because students could email their professor while simultaneously viewing assignment descriptions and lecture slides. The shortcut allowed for students to create an email without leaving their current page. Over 70% of users, utilized this tool as they navigated through Quercus.

Email Shortcut Icon Change

In the final prototype, the email icon was changed to the Outlook logo (UTORmail). The icon was changed for brand recognition. Users saw the Outlook logo and immediately understood the UTORmail integration.

Prefilled Information

Students found emailing their professor a lengthy and redundant process.In our second prototype, we added prefilled information such as course codes, professors’ emails and teaching assistant emails. By adding this feature, the user flow is shortened immensely. Also by automating this information, there is less room for error (i.e spelling mistakes).

Quercus Calendar

Calendar Shortcut

The calendar shortcut allowed for users to input a task into the calendar, while still viewing another page. Therefore, students could view their course page and simultaneously input an assignment deadline into the Calendar.

Academic Course Buttons

In our redesign of the Calendar, we made sure all assignment deadlines were shown and up-to-date. Previous interviewers complained that Quercus was unreliable because of this issue.

Furthermore, the colour-coded academic buttons at the top of the calendar act as shortcuts. By simply clicking on one of the buttons, users could input a unique course-related task. 50% of users, interacted with these buttons when asked to input a course-related activity (i.e. group meeting).

Collapsible Navigation

The collapsible navigation was introduced for accessibility. Some users found the Calendar too small to see certain dates. For that reason, the collapsible navigation increased the size of the Calendar.

Final Prototype

The final prototype was an accumulation of all the iterations due to each user takeaway.

Conclusion

Self-Reflection

This project helped me to realize the importance of the user. I learned to not only make design changes based on aesthetics but more importantly purpose. Conducting in-depth analyses of each iteration became a crucial habit as we redesigned Quercus.

Project Limitations and Next Steps

If my group and I were to collaborate on this project again, I would love to conduct user testing to a larger pool of diverse students. Unfortunately, for this project we were only able to gather students from the Communication Culture Information & Technology program. It would have been interesting and helpful to also see the pain points of Science, Business, Visual Arts University of Toronto students.

The next steps of this project include improving all functionalities of the Quercus system. Students thoroughly enjoyed the interactive Calendar and integrated UTORmail Inbox, it would be highly rewarding to also improve the Course Evaluation and Dashboard functionalities.
